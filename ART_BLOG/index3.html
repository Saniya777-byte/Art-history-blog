About Impressionism

Impressionism was an influential 19th-century art movement that emerged in France during the 1860s and 1870s.

It marked a radical departure from traditional academic painting by emphasizing the artist's perception of a scene rather than precise details or historical subjects. It focused on capturing light, atmosphere, and momentary impressions of everyday life.

Loose, visible brushstrokes gave a sense of spontaneity. Artists often used short, broken strokes of color rather than smooth blending..

Emphasis on natural light and its changing qualities. Use of pure, unmixed colors applied side by side to create vibrancy.

Everyday life, landscapes, urban scenes, and leisure activities. Avoided historical, mythological, or religious themes typical of academic art.

  • 1863: Édouard Manet exhibited Luncheon on the Grass (Le Déjeuner sur l'herbe) at the Salon des Refusés, challenging academic traditions and paving the way for Impressionism.
  • 1867–1869: Claude Monet and Pierre-Auguste Renoir painted together outdoors, developing techniques that captured light and color in natural settings (La Grenouillère).
  • 1870–1871:The Franco-Prussian War disrupted artistic activity. Monet moved to London, where he was influenced by J.M.W. Turner’s atmospheric works..
  • 1872: Monet painted Impression, Sunrise, depicting the port of Le Havre. This work later gave the movement its name.
  • 1899–1904:Monet painted his iconic Water Lilies series at his garden in Giverny, transitioning into a more abstract style.
